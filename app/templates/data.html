Cereals dataset


The cereal dataset includes average straw yield per hectares in some of the shires in Western Australia between 2006 and 2010, together with the total residue in each shire. This has been sourced from the Australian Government National map. At the moment, data are available only for 25 shires in WA.

Soil pH dataset


The pH of the soil is a measure of acidity or alkalinity. It basically indicates how ‘sour or sweet’ the soil is. The pH scale ranges from 0 to 14, with 7 being neutral. Values below 7 indicate an acid soil, and above 7, alkaline. Soil pH determines the nutrient availability to plants. Soils are more alkaline near the limestone-based coastal sands. Soils further inland, and most agricultural areas, are naturally acidic. Typically, Western Australian soils have a pH range between 4 and 8.5, (https://www.agric.wa.gov.au/soil-ph-and-plant-health?page=0%2C0). This dataset is a CSV file from an Australian Government dataset which reports soil pH measured for 1315 samples collected across Australia, with relative coordinates of the station.

Rainfall dataset


Rainfall contributes to a soil’s acidity. Water (H₂O) combines with carbon dioxide (CO₂) to form a weak acid — carbonic acid (H₂CO₃). The weak acid ionizes, releasing hydrogen (H⁺) and bicarbonate (HCO₃). The released hydrogen ions replace the calcium ions held by soil colloids, causing the soil to become acidic. The displaced calcium (Ca⁺⁺) ions combine with the bicarbonate ions to form calcium bicarbonate, which, being soluble, is leached from the soil. The net effect is increased soil acidity, (https://www.cropnutrition.com/nutrient-management/soil-ph). Rainfall also contribute to a reduced water consumption from aquifers and catchments. The rainfall dataset has been retrieved from the department of primary industries and regional development of Western Australia, through API endpoint. The dataset consist of the total rain in mm collected in 226 stations in Western Australia. Coordinates, station name, datetime, rainfall period are also available within the dataset.

Because the rain and soil datasets only provide coordinates and not shire name, to be able to merge the data a local government area dataset for WA in geojson format with polygons and locations has been used. The Shapely Python library was able to extract the lat and long from the 2 datasets and check them against areas in the geojson file.
